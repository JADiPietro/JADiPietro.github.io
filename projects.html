Waves of PDMS: Recreating the Great Wave Off Kanagawa Through Photothermal Patterning

Scientific Process/Description

The SEM image shows photothermally patterned polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) and carbon black composite, with the visualization enhanced through colorization in Blender. The patterning process was achieved using a 455 nm laser engraver, where carbon black acted as a photothermal agent to enable localized heating and spatially controlled crosslinking which leads to the formation of micro-patterns within the material. In this image a simple line was drawn through the material at a high laser power, leading to the flake structures resembling waves on the surface. Unlike conventional soft lithography or photolithography, this photothermal approach eliminates the need for masks and is a scalable and flexible method for patterning polymer surfaces.

Artist's Statement

While viewing this sample with SEM, I was interested in the chaotic flake structures that emerge at higher laser powers. To me these features resembled waves cresting in a uniform direction. The randomness of the flakes remind me of rough seas and the movement seen in the famous print, "The Great Wave off Kanagawa". To enhance this effect, I colorized the grayscale SEM image in Blender using a palette similar to traditional Japanese woodblock prints. The outer edges of the feature are colored with warm yellow and red tones, while the wave-like flakes are shaded in blue hues to mimic the movement of water. Much like the rough seas in Hokusai's famous print, these features capture a sense of fluidity and motion formed by the interaction of heat and material.

Photothermal Patterning of PDMS

Chemistry • 2024

The surface patterning of polymers is an important approach to enhancing material properties for a large variety of applications. Due to the formation of irreversible crosslinks however, thermoset polymers tend to be challenging to pattern. In this paper we present a novel method of patterning a commonly used thermoset polymer, polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), through controlled photothermal curing. We show that by incorporating 0.05% carbon black by weight into PDMS and moving a continuous wave-based laser engraver over the surface in a snake pattern, we can photothermally generate micron-scale surface features, and that these patterns can be controlled through laser parameters. Finally, we show that the photothermally patterned PDMS surfaces undergo changes in the optical properties as a result of patterning.
